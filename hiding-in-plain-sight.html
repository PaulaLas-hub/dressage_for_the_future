Hiding in Plain Sight

Chronic lip stretching

May 24, 2026
← All Essays

Originally published on Substack

There is something about the horse's mouth that we have all learned not to see. Not because it is hidden, and not because it is subtle, but because it is everywhere and there is no rule to stop its exploitation.

We talk about contact as if it is something the horse receives — a line, a connection, a conversation. We speak of soft hands, elastic reins, and acceptance. The language is familiar, and it is repeated so often that it begins to feel unquestionable.

But if you stop for a moment and look — not at the movement, not at the test, not at the outline — but simply at the corner of the mouth, something else begins to come into view.

In the unbitted horse, the mouth is a soft structure. The lips are closed. The lower lip hangs independently, mobile and free to move. When you measure it — even crudely, even in pixels — the pattern becomes clear. Without a bit, the distance from the poll to the lower lip sits at roughly seven times the distance from the commissures to the lower lip. It is a long system, ending in a soft, mobile structure.

Nothing is being held in place, and nothing is being organised around it. The endpoint is soft, mobile, and free to resolve.

Now look at the ridden horse. Not the trot, not the frame, not the score. Just the mouth.

The commissures are no longer soft; they are stretched up and back. The lips cannot close. They are stretched, sometimes parted, often prevented from returning to their natural position. This is not a difference of conformation.

This is the part that is hardest to see, because it contradicts what we have been taught. We are told that the horse accepts the bit, that contact is received, that stillness at the mouth is a sign of relaxation. But stillness is not the same as neutrality, and a structure that cannot return to baseline is not relaxed. It is held.

If you look closely at the corner of the mouth, you can see how this happens. The tissue is not simply under tension. It is compressed, lifted, and displaced. The lips are caught between the bit and the surrounding structures. The commissure becomes a point of pressure rather than a soft curve.

This is not a line of communication. It is a point the horse cannot easily resolve.

And when a system cannot resolve something locally, it does what all biological systems do. It reorganises around it. The jaw stiffens. The poll changes. The neck follows. The body begins to organise itself in relation to a fixed point at the mouth — not because the horse is "using the contact," but because it has to.

This is what makes the image so difficult to unsee once you have seen it. Nothing about it is hidden. It is not buried in data or confined to laboratory conditions. It is present in the most photographed, most observed, and most judged part of the horse.

Hiding in plain sight.

If there is a single shift that follows from this, it is a simple one. The question is no longer how the rider creates better contact, but what happens to the horse when the mouth cannot return to itself.

Once that question is asked honestly, the rest becomes much harder to ignore.
