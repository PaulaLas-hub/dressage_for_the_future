How to Create Engagement in Dressage

It doesn't start with the hindquarters

May 20, 2026
← All Essays

Originally published on Substack

As with many words in dressage, engagement has come to mean the visible mechanics of the body — hindquarters stepping under, elevation, contained power. But this definition is incomplete. Because the same physical picture can be produced by two entirely different internal states.

One is Engagement.

The other is Defence.

This is where the confusion begins. We have become very good at producing the appearance of engagement: active hind legs, expressive movement, energy that looks contained and powerful. But none of these things tell us what is happening inside the horse. A horse can move like that — impressively, even brilliantly — and still be operating in a defensive state.

Defensive movement is not laziness or resistance. It is the body organising itself under pressure. It is what happens when the horse is trying to cope, to stabilise, to protect itself in an environment it cannot fully predict or control. The muscles activate, the limbs move, the energy builds — but it is not offered freely. It is driven by tension, not curiosity.

For centuries, dressage has focused almost entirely on shaping the body, without distinguishing clearly between these two states. Not because horsemen didn't care, but because they didn't have the framework to recognise the difference. The system evolved to produce the picture. Whether that picture came from engagement or defence was largely invisible — and therefore largely ignored.

You can see the problem more clearly if you step outside horses for a moment. Imagine a six-year-old child placed into the same lesson every day: the same structure, the same questions, the same expected answers, for hours at a time. No variation. No autonomy. No curiosity. The child may comply. They may even perform well. But internally, they are no longer engaged — they are enduring.

Now bring that back into the arena. The same circles. The same transitions. The same corrections. The same expectations. And then we ask: why isn't the horse engaged?

When Xenophon wrote that a horse should look forward to the work and take pleasure in the exercise, he was not describing obedience or suppression. He was describing a state in which the horse is mentally present — interested, willing, participating.

That is engagement.

And it is much harder to create than its opposite.

Because true engagement cannot be imposed from the outside. It cannot be manufactured through more leg, more collection, more control. Those tools can produce movement — but they can just as easily produce defence.

Real engagement begins in the mind. It requires variation, purpose, and a sense of predictability that allows the horse to organise itself without constant interference. You see it most clearly outside the arena — on a trail, heading somewhere, with a horse that is attentive, forward-thinking, and focused. In that state, something shifts.

The body follows the mind.

The hindquarters engage. The movement becomes expressive. The energy becomes self-generated — not because it is extracted, but because it is offered.

This is the inversion dressage has largely missed. We have spent centuries trying to create engagement from the outside in. But engagement is an inside-out process. And when we fail to recognise the difference between engagement and defence, we risk rewarding the very thing we are trying to avoid.

Until we understand engagement in contrast to its opposite, we will continue to produce horses that look engaged — while moving in defence.
