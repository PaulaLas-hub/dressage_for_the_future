The Faulty Premise

If He Only Had a Brain

Modern dressage rests on a premise so outdated it collapses the moment it is named: the horse was never meant to have a mind.

The discipline was built during a period when horses were not understood as cognitive beings. They were machines of weight and force. Their bodies were studied; their minds were dismissed. This was not a fringe belief — it was the dominant scientific worldview of the 17th, 18th, and 19th centuries.

René Descartes articulated the doctrine explicitly in the 1630s–1640s, a doctrine that shaped European science for more than a century. In The Passions of the Soul (1649) and the posthumous Treatise on Man (1664), he wrote:

“And this proves not merely that the beasts have less reason than men, but that they have none at all.”

From this single sentence flowed an entire system of practice. If animals had no consciousness, then vivisection was justified, force-based training was normalised, and the very idea of animal agency became irrelevant.

The “animal-machine” doctrine solved a problem for early modern science: it allowed researchers to study bodies without confronting minds. And it solved a problem for military and agricultural systems: it allowed humans to treat horses as instruments of force, not as thinking partners.

No cognition required.

The Military Origin of Dressage

Dressage did not emerge from art. It emerged from war.

A horse was valuable only if its power could be controlled. With wars looming and cavalry functioning as the decisive force of the era, speed and reliability mattered more than welfare or understanding.

François Baucher’s 1842 Méthode d’équitation basée sur de nouveaux principes was written in this context. His premise makes the worldview unmistakable:

“The horse, like all organized beings, is possessed of a weight and force peculiar to himself.”

Instinctive forces came from within the horse. Transmitted forces came from the rider. Training was the process of overriding the former with the latter.

“The education of the horse consists of the complete subjection of his powers… by annulling all resistances.”

This was not philosophy. It was logistics.

France needed cavalry officers mounted quickly. Tens of thousands of horses were enlisted. A method that was fast, repeatable, and force-based was not just useful — it was essential. Baucher’s system delivered exactly that.

His manual was so effective at producing obedient horses in minimal time that it spread across Europe and became embedded in equitation systems that are still recognised today as “classical.”

The FEI’s Accidental Admission

Nearly a century later, when the FEI formalised international dressage competition, it revealed — unintentionally — the fragility of the system it inherited.

In the FEI Dressage Rules, Article 400 states:

“The FEI established an International Dressage Event in 1929 in order to preserve the Equestrian Art from the abuses to which it can be exposed and to preserve it in the purity of its principles, so that such principles of the discipline could be handed down intact to future generations of Athletes.”

This is meant as reassurance. Instead, it is an admission.

The FEI openly acknowledges that dressage is vulnerable to “abuses,” and that its goal is to preserve the “purity” of principles developed in an era when horses were considered machines. The organisation is not preserving harmony. It is preserving a worldview.

The context has been forgotten. The premise has not.

The Premise Did Not Disappear

The idea that the horse does not think was never formally removed from dressage. It was simply reworded.

Submission became “acceptance.”
Control became “contact.”
Obedience became “harmony.”

But the structure did not change.

The horse is still expected to yield to pressures it does not control, to respond to signals it did not define, and to operate within a system where the rider determines both the question and the answer.

A thinking animal has been placed inside a system designed for a non-thinking one.

And the conflict between those two realities has never been resolved.

Why This Still Matters

The problem is not only historical. It is structural.

The system that emerged from this worldview still asks the horse to do something that cannot be resolved.

It asks for:

  • Forward impulsion
  • Continuous contact
  • Collection

At the same time.

But these are not complementary. They are contradictory.

The Mechanical Conflict

Collection requires the horse to shift weight back.

Forward into contact shifts weight forward.

These are opposing directions.

No refinement changes that.

The Cognitive Conflict

Learning requires clarity.

A signal.
A response.
A release.

In dressage, the horse is asked to go forward — while being restricted at the same time.

There is no clear answer available.

So the horse does not learn.

It adapts.

The Result

  • Horses are tense
  • Riders are constantly adjusting
  • “On the aids” means never fully right

Because the system never resolves.

The Context That Cannot Be Ignored

Dressage inherited a system built on the denial of animal cognition, the normalisation of force, and the assumption that resistance is a mechanical defect.

These ideas were not incidental. They were the foundation.

Dressage was built for an animal that did not think, did not feel, and did not possess agency.

That animal never existed.

And a system built on a non-existent creature cannot be made humane through refinement. It must be questioned at its root.
