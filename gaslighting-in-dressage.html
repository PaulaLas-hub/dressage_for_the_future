Gaslighting in Dressage: Don't Believe Your Eyes

Apr 27, 2026
← All Essays

Originally published on Substack

There comes a point where the problem is no longer ignorance.

It is denial.

We are told, again and again, that modern dressage celebrates the happy athlete. We are told these horses are relaxed, willing, confident, proud in their work. We are told what we are seeing is harmony.

And yet the public can see otherwise.

Eyes wide. Mouths strained. Nostrils tight. Necks rigid. Tails lashing.

Expressions of conflict, tension, shutdown, or distress.

Then comes the script:

It's only a moment in time. You don't understand high-performance sport. That horse loves his job. That is concentration. That is power. That is brilliance.

No.

That is gaslighting.

Gaslighting is the attempt to make people distrust their own perception in order to protect someone else's narrative.

And that is exactly what happens in dressage.

An ordinary person looks at a horse's face and feels discomfort. They sense strain. They sense something wrong. They recognise the universal language of tension that every mammal wears in its body.

Then the institution tells them:

You didn't see what you saw. You felt wrong. Experts know better. Trust the scoreboard. Trust the medals. Trust the commentary.

But the eyes do not lie so easily.

You do not need a judging licence to recognise fear, conflict, bracing, or suppression. You do not need a federation handbook to notice when an animal looks overwhelmed. Human beings are deeply attuned to facial expression and body language. It is one of our oldest survival skills.

What dressage asks the public to do is override that instinct in service of prestige.

That is why so many people feel uneasy watching it, even if they cannot fully explain why.

Because somewhere underneath the technical language, they know:

A horse can perform and still be distressed. Obedience is not the same as wellbeing. Stillness is not the same as peace. Brilliance can be built on suffering.

And everyone inside the system knows it too.

The judges know what conflict looks like. The trainers know what pressure creates. The riders know the difference between softness and containment. The spectators know what they are seeing.

But the machine runs on collective pretence.

So the public is told not to trust itself.

The horse pays the price.

The greatest threat to this system is not scandal, protest, or science.

It is honesty.

The moment enough people say: that horse does not look happy, that does not look harmonious, I will believe my eyes —

The spell breaks.

The emperor is naked.

And everyone knows it.
