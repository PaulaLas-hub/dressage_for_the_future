Something Huge Just Happened

Carl Hester admitted it

Apr 13, 2026
← All Essays

Originally published on Substack

"The horse needs to push onto the bit."

That has massive implications.

When a Dressage Icon Says the Quiet Part Out Loud

At a U.S. Masterclass this week, Carl Hester told the audience:

"To get to Grand Prix, the horse needs to push onto the bit — not be light. Light is not self-carriage in a young horse."

This is not a harmless training preference. This is a public endorsement of sustained, non-contingent bit pressure on young horses.

And according to the FEI's own rules, that is misuse.

The FEI explicitly prohibits the thing he told riders to do

The FEI Code of Conduct states that horses must not be subjected to training methods that cause force, pain, or fear. The FEI Dressage Rules prohibit sustained or excessive rein pressure, aggressive riding, riding behind the vertical for prolonged periods, and any action that causes pain or distress.

A young horse being driven "onto the bit" cannot resolve the pressure. There is no release. There is no learning. There is only coercion.

By FEI standards, that is misuse of the bit.

Learning theory is even clearer: pressure without release is abuse

ISES (International Society for Equitation Science) defines ethical negative reinforcement as: light pressure, a clear response, an immediate release.

ISES warns that constant rein tension and non-contingent pressure produce conflict behaviours, stress, confusion, and learned helplessness.

A young horse cannot understand a "firm contact." It can only endure it. That is not training. That is coercion.

Welfare science shows exactly what this produces

Decades of peer-reviewed research show that sustained rein tension causes oral lesions, tongue compression, poll torque, elevated cortisol, behind-the-vertical postures, conflict behaviours, and shutdown and appeasement.

AWIN (Animal Welfare Indicators Network) classifies these as markers of compromised welfare. These outcomes are not anomalies. They are the predictable result of unresolvable pressure.

So when a global figure tells riders to apply unresolvable pressure…

…he is not just giving a training tip. He is normalising a practice that violates FEI welfare rules, contradicts learning theory, produces measurable pain, creates the very welfare problems the sport claims are "rare," and erodes equestrianism's social licence.

Dressage has spent years insisting that blue tongues are freak accidents. But when the foundational instruction is to drive young horses into sustained bit pressure, the outcomes are not freakish at all. They are systemic.

This is the moment the sport must decide what it stands for

You cannot claim to uphold FEI welfare principles while teaching riders to apply the exact form of pressure the FEI defines as unacceptable.

You cannot claim to value self-carriage while instructing riders to manufacture it through force.

You cannot claim blue tongues are anomalies when the training method that produces them is being taught on stage.

This is not a small slip. This is not a misphrasing. This is the quiet part said out loud.

And now the sport has to decide whether it will keep pretending not to hear it.
