The Soft Toy

On the difference between welfare for the horse and comfort for the owner.

Jun 25, 2026
← All Essays

An email landed in my inbox this week advertising a new range of luxury soft toys for horses.

My first thought was: WTAF?

Not because someone had designed another horse product. The horse industry designs products every day. It was because I realised what these toys actually represent.

They are not really about horses.

They are about us.

The marketing is clever. Your horse is bored. Your horse is lonely. Your horse needs entertainment. Hang one of these soft toys in the stable and you’ll enrich its life. It sounds compassionate. It is welfare signalling. People walk past the stable and think: Wow, she really loves her horse. He’s spoiled. He lives like a king.

But stop for a moment and ask a different question.

Why would someone buy a soft toy for a horse in the first place?

Obligate needs

There are some needs that are optional.

There are others that are obligate.

An obligate need is not a luxury. It is not enrichment. It is not something that simply makes life a little nicer. It is a biological requirement.

Think about a newborn baby. During the first weeks of life, human infants are obligate nose breathers. Block the nose completely and the baby cannot simply decide to breathe through its mouth. Biology doesn’t negotiate.

Horses have obligate needs too.

Obligate needs are different from preferences because they are not negotiable. A horse cannot simply adapt to life without social contact, movement or foraging any more than a fish can adapt to life without water. It may survive. It may even appear to cope. But coping is not the same as fulfilling the biological conditions it evolved to require.

Horses are obligate social animals. They are obligate foragers. They are obligate movers. Their bodies, brains and behaviour evolved around spending most of the day walking, grazing, interacting with other horses and responding to an ever-changing environment.

These are not preferences.

They are the foundations of being a horse.

The invisible deprivation

The problem is that some forms of deprivation are obvious. If a horse is starving, everyone can see it. Its ribs protrude. Its muscles waste away. Nobody responds by buying it a toy because everyone understands what the horse actually needs.

Food.

Confinement is different.

A horse standing alone in a stable may look glossy, beautifully rugged and impeccably groomed. It may even be overweight. To most people, it appears perfectly cared for.

Yet that horse may be deprived of movement, companionship, choice, exploration and the opportunity to live a species-typical life. Because these losses do not leave bones sticking through the skin, they are easy to overlook.

So instead, we invent products.

Stable toys.

Boredom breakers.

Flavoured licks.

Mirrors.

Novelty gadgets.

The existence of these products tells us something important. They exist because something is missing. If the horse’s environment already fulfilled its biological needs, there would be no market for products designed to compensate for their absence.

Who benefits?

But these products serve another purpose as well.

They make the owner feel better.

Not by fundamentally changing the horse’s life, but by changing how the owner feels about the horse’s life.

The purchase becomes an emotional intervention. It says: I have done something. I am a caring owner.

Meanwhile, the horse remains in the same stable. The same confinement. The same restricted movement. The same lack of social interaction.

The horse’s life is unchanged.

The owner’s discomfort has been eased.

That is why these products make me uncomfortable. Not because I object to kindness. Quite the opposite. I object to confusing comfort for the owner with welfare for the horse.

This is what happens whenever we replace an obligate biological need with a consumer product.

The horse doesn’t need another object hanging from the stable wall. It needs the things evolution designed it to need: more movement, more forage, more horses, more choice and more telos.

None of those can be bought from a catalogue. Most require changing management, not shopping.

Perhaps that is why these products are so attractive. Buying something is easier than questioning the environment itself.

The greatest welfare intervention is rarely the most profitable one.
It is opening the stable door.
The next time someone tries to sell you a product that promises to improve your horse’s welfare, ask one simple question:

Is this meeting one of my horse’s obligate needs — or is it helping me feel better about the fact that it isn’t?

Those are not the same thing.

A stable toy cannot replace movement. It cannot replace companions. It cannot replace foraging. It cannot replace freedom.

The greatest welfare intervention has never been hanging another object in the stable.

It has always been giving the horse more telos.
