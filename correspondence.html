Correspondence

Public correspondence with governing bodies, organisations, and stakeholders including Equestrian Australia, the FEI, DAFF, Dressage NSW, NCEC, and the RSPCA.

These are real exchanges — sent, received, and published here in full. They document what happens when you ask the governing bodies of equestrian sport to account for the welfare implications of their rules.

The question put to each institution is essentially the same: you claim to care about horse welfare. What does that mean, in practice, when a rule requires a bit?

Why Publish Correspondence?

Debates about animal welfare in equestrian sport are largely conducted behind closed doors. Policies are set, rules are maintained, and positions are defended in contexts that are rarely visible to the people those decisions affect — riders, horse owners, welfare advocates, and the public. The horses themselves have no voice in these processes at all.

Dressage for the Future publishes correspondence with governing bodies, regulatory agencies, welfare organisations, and other stakeholders as a matter of transparency. Readers should be able to examine the original questions, the evidence and reasoning offered, and the responses received — or not received — and reach their own conclusions. A summary of someone else's position is no substitute for the primary record.

Publishing this correspondence serves three purposes: documentation, accountability, and the conditions necessary for informed discussion. It is not intended as a vehicle for personal criticism, institutional embarrassment, or advocacy by implication. Where an organisation has responded substantively, that response is included in full. Where no response has been received, that too is part of the record.

All correspondence published here was written and sent in good faith, with the expectation that welfare questions of this kind merit serious engagement. It is offered here in the same spirit.

NCEC — Northern Canberra Equestrian Club

December 2025 · NCEC Event Secretary
Freya & Leo — bitless request at the Christmas event

An exchange with NCEC Event Secretary Jacqui Knobel in the days before the club's Christmas dressage event (7 December 2025). Paula requested permission to ride Freya and Leo bitless — either HC or to be eliminated after the test so feedback could still be received. The committee declined on the basis of EA rules and insurance obligations. The exchange includes a notable inconsistency: the event notice permitted boots and bandages (usually prohibited in dressage) as a Christmas concession, while refusing the bitless request on regulatory grounds.

24 February 2026 · NCEC Secretary
Request for HC permission at the March 29 competition

Following receipt of the club's March event schedule, Paula wrote to NCEC Secretary Lisa Pluis requesting permission to ride HC in the Prelim and Novice tests in a plain leather direct-pull bitless bridle. The request was framed as a contained, low-risk step: judges to assess the test under normal criteria, stewarding unaffected, and the bridle available for inspection. Equestrian Australia

6 March 2026 · Equestrian Australia · Dressage NSW · NCEC
Bitless dressage pilot proposal and request for permission to compete

A formal proposal sent jointly to Equestrian Australia, Dressage NSW, and NCEC Dressage, setting out the case for a limited HC pilot to allow bitless combinations to be observed under standard judging conditions. The proposal drew on the Dutch federation's rules (KNHS Article 147), which permit dressage tests bitless up to ZZ-Zwaar level, and argued that judging based on the training scale is hardware-neutral. EA's Sport Development Officer Caiwen Cusworth replied on 10 March advising that approval could not be provided under current rules, even for HC participation.

28 March 2026 · Equestrian Australia National Dressage Committee
Formal member request for a rule change

Following EA's refusal to grant an exemption, Paula submitted a formal rule change request to the National Dressage Committee. The submission argued that the mandatory bit rule lacks a welfare basis proportionate to the evidence of bit-related injury, that EA already permits bitless bridles in jumping and eventing, and that international precedent (notably the KNHS) demonstrates that dressage can be judged without reference to specific equipment. The submission requested that the Committee review the Equipment Annex, amend the definition of contact to be hardware-neutral, and publish a substantive written response addressing whether the bit is accepted as a welfare risk.

4 April 2026 · Equestrian Australia
Request for definition: "alteration of sensation" in EA policy

A follow-up to the rule change submission raising a specific interpretive question about EA's stated policy that "any attempt to alter the normal sensation of the horse for performance enhancement will not be tolerated." Paula argued that without a defined baseline, this principle is unenforceable — all ridden equipment interacts with the horse's sensory system, and the prohibition on bitless bridles sits in direct tension with a rule that nominally restricts the alteration of sensation. The letter asked how "normal sensation" is defined, how stewards assess the distinction, and what evidence supports the current equipment rules.

DAFF — Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry

14 February 2026 · Department of Agriculture, Fisheries and Forestry
Formal submission: Operationalising Sentience and Bodily Integrity under the National Statement on Animal Welfare (2024)

A formal submission to DAFF seeking clarification on how animal sentience is operationalised under the National Statement on Animal Welfare (2024), specifically in relation to bodily integrity and control methods involving mucosal sensory tissue. The submission argues that mucosal and densely innervated regions — the mouth, eyes, nostrils, and related tissues — are neurologically distinct from external skin and must be treated as such in welfare frameworks. It asks four specific questions: whether the Statement recognises mucosal tissue as ethically distinct; whether bodily integrity is assessed beyond visible injury; how measurable welfare outcomes are defined where control methods involve sustained pressure on internal sensory anatomy; and how sentience materially affects the permissibility of invasive control devices. The submission is species-neutral — it addresses the structural ethical framework, not equestrian practice specifically.

RSPCA NSW

10 April 2026 · RSPCA NSW Campaigns Team
Welfare clarification request: bitless bridles in dressage

A letter to the RSPCA NSW Campaigns Team following a phone enquiry, seeking clarification on whether the use of bitless bridles in dressage is consistent with RSPCA welfare principles, whether permitting riders to choose bitless equipment would be viewed as a positive welfare development, and whether Paula could accurately represent this position when engaging with governing bodies. The letter was not a request for RSPCA to take on a campaign, but for welfare guidance relevant to ongoing correspondence with Equestrian Australia and Dressage NSW.

FEI — Fédération Équestre Internationale

16 May 2026 · FEI Veterinary and Dressage Departments
Request for guidance: bit position and cheekpiece adjustment

A letter to the FEI's veterinary and dressage departments (addressed to Göran Åkerström, cc Ronan Murphy and Caterina Termine) asking whether the FEI has any formal standard for bit height — specifically the degree of lip tension or wrinkling at the commissures considered acceptable at competition. Given growing research into oral lesions at the lip commissures, bars, and buccal mucosa in sport horses, the letter asked whether stewards are trained to assess bit position, and how current FEI guidance on bit use aligns with emerging evidence on rein tension and tissue injury.
