A Framework for Equine Welfare

Teleonomic
Fulfilment

A new framework for understanding equine welfare.

Most welfare assessments ask whether a horse is healthy, comfortable, or performing well. They measure absence — the absence of injury, the absence of obvious distress. Teleonomic Fulfilment asks a different question.

Can this horse fulfil the biological life it evolved to live?
Section One

What is Teleonomic Fulfilment?

Teleonomic Fulfilment is a framework for understanding equine welfare that begins with a simple premise: every horse carries within its biology a specific kind of life it evolved to live. Welfare, understood through this framework, is not simply the absence of pain or disease. It is the presence of genuine opportunity to express that evolved life — to move, to forage, to bond, to explore, and to make choices that have real consequences.

This matters because the gap between a horse that is not suffering and a horse that is genuinely flourishing is wider than most welfare systems currently measure. A horse can pass every clinical check and still be living in conditions that deny it almost everything its biology was shaped to do. Teleonomic Fulfilment provides the vocabulary and the architecture to see that gap — and to take it seriously.

Section Two

Why another framework?

Existing welfare science has transformed how we think about animals. This framework does not replace it. But it asks a question the others do not.

The Five Freedoms, the Five Domains, and the frameworks built on them have done extraordinary work. They gave animal welfare science a language, a method, and a moral seriousness it had lacked. We owe them a great deal.

But these frameworks were principally designed to evaluate suffering. They ask whether an animal is harmed, hungry, diseased, fearful, or in pain. Even the more recent frameworks that incorporate positive experience — asking not just "is the animal suffering?" but "is the animal experiencing anything good?" — still operate on a spectrum anchored at the negative end.

Teleonomic Fulfilment asks a different kind of question entirely.

Not "is this horse harmed?" but "is this horse able to be the kind of animal it evolved to be?" This is not a question about the minimum conditions for survival or the avoidance of suffering. It is a question about the full expression of equine life — and whether the conditions we have created allow for it.

It is also a question that most of the horses in human care would currently fail. Not because their owners are cruel, but because the systems we have built around horses were designed for human convenience, sport, and tradition — not for the biology of the animal at their centre.

Section Three

What is Telos?

The framework is built on a single Greek word. Understanding it changes how you see every horse.

Telos is the evolved biological nature of a species — the set of functions, capacities, and drives that define what kind of creature it is and what conditions it needs to express that nature fully. It is not a goal imposed from outside. It arises from within, shaped by millions of years of natural selection. The philosopher Bernard Rollin called it "the pigness of the pig, the dogness of the dog."

For horses, telos is written into the body. The horse evolved as a wide-ranging grassland animal in stable, bonded social groups — grazing almost continuously while moving, navigating complex relationships, reading its environment through sophisticated sensory systems. These are not preferences. They are expressions of what this animal is. To constrain them is to prevent the horse from being the kind of animal it evolved to be.

Definition

τέλος (telos) — the inherent biological nature of an organism as shaped by evolutionary history; the functions, drives, and capacities without which that organism cannot fully be what it is. Related to teleonomy — the principle that organisms pursue biological goals arising from within rather than externally imposed. This concept forms the theoretical backbone of the Teleonomic Fulfilment framework.

Section Four

The Obligate Needs

Not preferences. Not enrichments. The biological conditions without which the horse's evolved systems cannot function as they evolved to.

Social Contact

Horses evolved in stable, bonded groups with continuous physical proximity and long-term social knowledge. Isolation and frequent group rotation are biologically costly in ways that most management systems do not account for.

Read concept → Movement

The horse's musculoskeletal, digestive, cardiovascular, and psychological systems all evolved around continuous movement. Self-directed locomotion is a foundational biological function, not recreational exercise.

Read concept → Foraging

Continuous selective grazing while moving integrates the locomotor, digestive, olfactory, and reward systems into a single evolved activity that cannot be replicated by scheduled feeding.

Read concept → Choice & Agency

The capacity to approach, withdraw, investigate, and control outcomes is a core evolved function in all sentient prey species. Its systematic removal does not produce a calmer horse — it produces an animal whose choices no longer matter.

Read concept → Exploration

Horses evolved in a changing home range. The neurological systems responsible for curiosity and investigation require novel input. A static environment does not merely bore the horse — it deprives these systems of their evolved function.

Read concept → Rest

REM sleep requires lateral recumbency, perceived safety, space, and companion proximity. Horses that cannot lie flat, or whose environment prevents deep rest, are not simply tired — they are neurologically compromised.

Section Five

The Nine Domains

Teleonomic Fulfilment is assessed across nine domains of evolved equine function. Together they describe the full architecture of what it means to be a horse.

01
Movement & Locomotion

Self-directed movement, including the capacity to reach full locomotor speed, across adequate space.

02
Foraging & Nutrition

Continuous selective grazing while moving, across varied forage, without prolonged gaps — the primary occupation of equine life.

03
Social Connection

Physical contact, mutual grooming, and long-term companionship within a stable social group.

04
Environmental Engagement

Weather, varied terrain, natural light cycles, and a changing sensory world — the inputs the horse's nervous system evolved to process.

05
Agency & Choice

The capacity to approach or withdraw, to initiate and terminate interactions, and to exert meaningful influence over one's life.

06
Rest & Recovery

The conditions necessary for deep sleep: lateral recumbency, perceived safety, adequate space, and the proximity of companion animals.

07
Affective Experience

Positive emotional states — curiosity, play, contentment — are biological signals that teleonomic systems are functioning as they evolved to.

08
Exploratory Behaviour

Neurological systems for investigation and curiosity require changing inputs; a static world leaves them without function.

09
Thermoregulatory Autonomy

The capacity to seek sun, shade, shelter, and social warmth — a self-regulatory function that requires environmental variation to operate.
